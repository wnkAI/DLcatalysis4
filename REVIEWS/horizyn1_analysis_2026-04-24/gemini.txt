### Idea 1: Learned Reaction Transformer (Replacing static DRFP)
*   **What to change:** Swap the static, hash-based `y_rxn` (DRFP) for a lightweight transformer over reaction SMILES (e.g., a frozen or LoRA-tuned ChemBERTa/RxnRoBERTa), mimicking Horizyn-1's reaction encoder.
*   **Why it's worth it:** DRFP relies on static substructure counting. A learned transformer captures dynamic atom-mapping, stereochemistry, and functional group transformations natively, which is critical for distinguishing subtle kcat differences in non-natural or promiscuous reactions.
*   **Trade-off:** High. Replaces a cheap 2048-d vector with a heavy self-attention forward pass. Substantially increases VRAM usage and risks overfitting on a ~30k regression dataset without massive pre-training. 
*   **Verdict:** **LATER.** The compute and engineering costs are too steep for a mid-version update unless DRFP is demonstrably bottlenecking your OOD substrate performance.

### Idea 2: Symmetric Contrastive Auxiliary Loss (InfoNCE)
*   **What to change:** Add an InfoNCE loss term explicitly aligning the unimodal enzyme embedding (`y_seq`) and the unimodal reaction embedding (`y_sub` + `y_rxn`) *before* they enter the cross-attention (`y_int3d`) and fusion MLP.
*   **Why it's worth it:** It acts as a powerful regularizer. Forcing the isolated encoders into a globally aligned shared latent space ensures that baseline catalytic compatibility is learned robustly, preventing the highly-parameterized multi-modal fusion layers from memorizing noise. Excellent for OOD generalization.
*   **Trade-off:** Low compute, moderate engineering. Requires implementing in-batch negative sampling and hyperparameter tuning to balance $\lambda_{contrastive}$ against your existing logcosh and pairwise ranking losses.
*   **Verdict:** **YES.** It maximizes the feature quality of your individual branches almost for free.

### Idea 3: Explicit Cosine Similarity Feature in Fusion
*   **What to change:** Project the independent enzyme and reaction embeddings into a shared dimension, compute their cosine similarity (Horizyn-1's exact retrieval metric), and feed this single scalar directly into your final regression MLP alongside the pooled vectors.
*   **Why it's worth it:** Horizyn-1 proves this dot-product is highly predictive of basic catalysis. Explicitly passing this "compatibility score" to the regression head provides a massive shortcut, freeing up the complex structural modules (`y_int3d`, `y_struct`) to focus entirely on precision kinetic fine-tuning rather than having to relearn basic binding feasibility.
*   **Trade-off:** Negligible. Costs a couple of linear layers, one dot-product, and practically zero engineering hours.
*   **Verdict:** **YES.** It is the cheapest, most direct way to steal Horizyn-1's core signal for your regression task.

### What NOT to copy: The Pure Dual-Encoder (Late-Interaction) Architecture
Do not abandon your deep cross-attention (`y_int3d`) or structural (`y_struct`) modules to adopt Horizyn-1's strictly independent encoders. Horizyn-1 forces a late-interaction (dot product only) bottleneck because they *must* pre-compute embeddings for ultra-fast, million-scale vector retrieval. We are doing precision regression on pre-selected pairs; dropping early-interaction structural modeling (like pocket-substrate distance matrices) would destroy the mechanistic and spatial inductive biases absolutely required to predict continuous `kcat/Km` values.

***

**Strongest recommendation:** Implement the InfoNCE auxiliary loss on your unimodal branches to force a globally aligned representation space, providing a strong regularization prior before your heavy cross-modal fusion takes over.
